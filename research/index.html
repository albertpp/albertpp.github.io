Research

I am a junior student at the Resource and Environmental Management Research Center affiliating to College of Urban and Environmental Sciences, Peking University.

 

#Research Focus

  • Regional transformation and transportation of pollutants like ammonia and mercury. This includes emission inventory compilation and transportation model development.
  • Analysis of emission trends associated with energy consumption and related policies, which requires methods like LMDI, I-O analysis, LCA and MFA analysis.
  • Every possible chance for field sampling.
  • Always fighting for new possibilities!

 

#Current Projects

  • Ammonia Emission Inventory Compilation

    (This work is currently being submitted to journal ES&T)

    While agriculture and livestock are usually considered the biggest contributors to ammonia emissions, there are satellite images and other agiculture-side model work that say something different. The ammonia emitted from industrial and waste disposal sectors in China is non-negligible, especially in dense-populated urban cities and provinces with heavy industries.

    Also, current ammonia emission inventories mostly use proxy data while point-source data is lacking. Our work provides a detailed high resolution industrial and waste disposal ammonia emission inventory with data from various economic and geographic databases and published lists from Chinese government.

 

  • LMDI Analysis of emission trends of ammonia

    This is a follow-up work of the first project. With detailed ammonia emissions from industrial and waste disposal sectors, we now have the chance to study how does this 3-fold increase come into place. With LMDI analysis together with some regression techniques obtained from Econometrics, we try to explain this increase from a political stand.

 

  • Glacial Mercury Release in Tibetan Plateau

    Often, we can hear saying like “glaciers are melting and results in those pollutants inside released”. But no one has ever answer how much of these released pollutants will be. We lay eyes on the Tibetan Plateau, the “Asian Water Tower”, and mercury, the most important heavy metal pollutants that also can travel long distance.

    By estimating the amount of glaciers melting and massive field sampling, we develop a method of calculating glacial mercury release from Tibetan Plateau, which can also be used for other estimation of heavy metal release from high mountain glaciers.
